Homework 1 - Pragmatic Programming and HTML

By Stephen Bothwell

Colleges, as institutions, are (or, at least, are supposed to be) dedicated to learning. Students take many a rigorous course with the aim of becoming proficient at some desirable skillset. After that, the envisioned ideal is for us to ship ourselves off into a career suited to our trained and tested repertoire. But this view of the development of fledgling adults into adept professionals is entirely too shortsighted, static, and simplistic. David Thomas and Andrew Hunt's The Pragmatic Programmer is very aware of this and seeks to curb that myopic perspective such that programmers do not stifle their own potential through arrogance, laziness, or ignorance.

As a second-semester senior at Xavier University, I have only one slew of classes to get through before I bid this wonderful place farewell. Such a realization is both exciting and saddening, but the question of what I shall do after my formal schooling is officially over has consistently haunted me. Graduate school is an attractive option, but I had very little time to think about applying with two theses over my head in the fall semester. As such, a year-long gap stands between me and my quest for some of the highest education that I could possibly attain. On top of this, there are certain subjects that I have not tackled yet that a person interested in the fields of AI and NLP would benefit from knowing in advance (i.e. multivariable calculus or probability theory). So, I have frequently considered the prospect of self-driven education. The obstacle looming before me, however, is how exactly I would tackle that self-guided learning such that I would not lose motivation and I would actually achieve some aptitude in my chosen areas of study.

In relation to my personal pursuits, Hunt and Thomas are helpful both in reinforcing my perspective that I should always learn as much as I can throughout my life and in providing practical methodologies toward actually providing myself with the learning that I desire. The tried-and-true fiscal metaphor of the "knowledge portfolio" drives their point home in many ways, as our post-graduation education could lend itself to a higher payout while we do business with businesses. However, the tip that struck me the most was that [l]earning an emerging technology before it becomes popular can be just as hard as finding an undervalued stock, but the payoff can be just as rewarding (Thomas and Hunt "Your Knowledge Portfolio"). While I am not a risk-taker, making advances in my own education by my own curiosity and sensibility seems to be beneficial whether what I gained from the process became profitable or not. Furthermore, as long as I can rationalize my independent training under the continued improvement of general skills, I see little reason not to branch out and to try to cultivate my own versatility and expertise. There are many methods to experiment with (i.e. learning new programming languages, using new environments, subscribing to journals, trade magazines, or periodicals, or meeting with local user groups), so I am not bereft of doable options, either. Indeed, despite not being at an institution devoted to education soon enough, I still have a plethora of reasons and of options in advancing my own prowess.


Works Cited
